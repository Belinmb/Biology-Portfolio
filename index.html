The Biological Journey of Pizza Energy The Solar Foundation: Where It All Begins

Every Friday night when I bite into my favorite pepperoni pizza, I'm consuming energy that began its journey 93 million miles away—in our sun. The wheat flour in the crust started as wheat plants swaying in vast fields, their leaves packed with chlorophyll molecules acting like tiny solar panels. Through photosynthesis, these plants captured sunlight energy and combined carbon dioxide from the air with water from their roots to create glucose: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + sunlight → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. This glucose became the foundation of the wheat kernels that would eventually become my pizza dough.

The tomatoes in the sauce followed a similar path, using photosynthesis in their green leaves to produce the sugars that made the fruit sweet and energy-rich. Even the pepperoni connects to this plant-powered chain—the pigs that became the meat spent their lives converting plant glucose from corn and soybeans into the fats and proteins in their muscle tissue.

From Farm to Fork: Processing and Consumption

The wheat was harvested, ground into flour, and mixed with water and yeast to create dough. The tomatoes were processed into sauce, and the milk from grass-fed cows became mozzarella cheese. When I take that first bite, my teeth break down the complex structure, but the real work begins in my digestive system. Enzymes in my saliva, stomach, and small intestine break the pizza into its component macromolecules: carbohydrates from the crust become simple sugars, fats from the cheese and pepperoni are broken into fatty acids, and proteins are reduced to amino acids.

The Cellular Powerhouse: ATP Production

Once these molecules enter my bloodstream, the real energy conversion begins in my cells' mitochondria—the cellular powerhouses. The glucose from the wheat undergoes cellular respiration in three main stages. First, glycolysis in the cytoplasm breaks glucose into pyruvate, producing a small amount of ATP. Next, the Krebs cycle in the mitochondria further processes these molecules, releasing carbon dioxide as a waste product. Finally, the electron transport chain uses oxygen I breathe to create the majority of ATP molecules—about 32-38 per glucose molecule.

This ATP becomes the universal energy currency of my body. Some powers the muscle contractions as I chew and digest, some fuels my brain as I savor the flavors, and some generates heat to keep my body temperature stable. In a beautiful cycle, I exhale the carbon dioxide produced by cellular respiration, returning it to the atmosphere where plants will use it again for photosynthesis, continuing this endless dance of energy transformation from sun to cell and back again.

Huntington's Disease: A Genetic Disorder Project
Section 1: Overview of the Disorder

Huntington's Disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that affects movement, cognition, and emotional regulation. This devastating condition typically manifests in mid-adulthood, though juvenile and late-onset forms exist. The disease is characterized by a triad of symptoms: involuntary choreiform movements (dance-like jerky motions), cognitive decline leading to dementia, and psychiatric disturbances including depression, anxiety, and personality changes.
The physical symptoms begin subtly with minor coordination problems and gradually progress to severe movement disorders. Patients develop characteristic writhing movements, difficulty walking, and problems with speech and swallowing. Cognitive symptoms include memory loss, difficulty concentrating, and impaired judgment. As the disease progresses, patients lose the ability to walk, talk, and care for themselves, with death typically occurring 15-20 years after symptom onset.
Huntington's Disease is caused by mutations in the HTT gene (formerly called IT15), located on chromosome 4 at position 4p16.3. This gene normally produces a protein called huntingtin, which plays crucial roles in nerve cell function, though its exact mechanisms are still being researched. The mutation involves an expansion of CAG trinucleotide repeats within the gene's first exon.
Section 2: Connection to the Central Dogma
The central dogma of molecular biology (DNA → RNA → Protein) is directly disrupted in Huntington's Disease through a specific type of mutation. The HTT gene contains a sequence of CAG repeats that normally ranges from 10-35 repeats in healthy individuals. However, in Huntington's Disease, this sequence expands to 36 or more repeats, with higher numbers correlating with earlier disease onset.
During transcription, the expanded CAG repeats in the DNA are faithfully copied into the mRNA. When translation occurs, each CAG codon codes for the amino acid glutamine, resulting in an abnormally long polyglutamine tract within the huntingtin protein. This trinucleotide repeat expansion represents a unique type of mutation distinct from simple substitutions, deletions, or insertions.
The mutant huntingtin protein with its expanded polyglutamine tract becomes toxic to neurons, particularly in the basal ganglia and cortex. The abnormal protein tends to misfold and aggregate, forming inclusion bodies within nerve cells. These aggregates disrupt normal cellular functions, impair protein degradation pathways, and ultimately lead to neuronal death. The longer the polyglutamine tract, the more severe the protein dysfunction and earlier the disease onset.
Section 3: Inheritance Pattern and Reflection
Huntington's Disease follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern, meaning only one copy of the mutated gene is needed to cause the disease. This has profound implications for families, as each child of an affected parent has a 50% chance of inheriting the condition. Unlike recessive disorders, the disease can appear in every generation, and both males and females are equally affected.
The condition also exhibits genetic anticipation, where the CAG repeat length tends to increase when passed from parent to child, particularly through paternal transmission. This can result in earlier onset and more severe symptoms in successive generations.
I chose Huntington's Disease because it perfectly illustrates several key genetic principles we've studied: dominant inheritance, anticipation, and the direct relationship between gene structure and protein function. The disease also highlights the ethical complexities of genetic testing, as individuals can know their genetic status decades before symptoms appear.
Understanding genetics has fundamentally changed my perspective on health and medicine. It demonstrates that many conditions aren't simply "caught" but are written into our genetic code from birth. This knowledge emphasizes the importance of genetic counseling, early intervention strategies, and the potential for future gene therapies. It also highlights how a single molecular change can have devastating consequences, underscoring the precision required in genetic research and therapy development.
The study of Huntington's Disease has contributed significantly to our understanding of protein folding, neurodegeneration, and potential therapeutic targets, making it a cornerstone condition in modern genetic medicine.

Ecosystem Analysis - Indianapolis Urban Park
Photo and Description

Photo: Downtown Indianapolis urban park showing mature deciduous trees in early fall colors, maintained grass areas, paved walking paths, and park benches.
Description: This temperate urban park ecosystem features a well-established canopy of mature deciduous trees with maintained turf grass ground cover and integrated recreational infrastructure, representing a managed forest-grassland ecosystem in an urban setting.
Species Identification
American Elm - Ulmus americana
Sugar Maple - Acer saccharum
Kentucky Bluegrass - Poa pratensis
American Robin - Turdus migratorius
Eastern Gray Squirrel - Sciurus carolinensis
Food Chain Web
Primary Food Chain: Kentucky Bluegrass (Poa pratensis) → Earthworms → American Robin (Turdus migratorius) → Cooper's Hawk (Accipiter cooperii)
Energy Flow: Solar energy is captured by grass through photosynthesis (producer level). Earthworms consume decomposing organic matter and grass roots (primary consumer level). American robins hunt earthworms on the lawn (secondary consumer level). Cooper's hawks prey on robins and other small birds (tertiary consumer level). Energy decreases at each trophic level following the 10% rule.
Species Interaction
Type: Mutualism
Species Involved: Sugar Maple (Acer saccharum) and Eastern Gray Squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis)
Explanation: This represents a seed dispersal mutualism. Gray squirrels collect and bury maple seeds (samaras) throughout the park as winter food caches. Both species benefit: squirrels gain stored food resources, while maples achieve seed dispersal when forgotten caches germinate in new locations, reducing parent-offspring competition and expanding the tree's range.
Reflection:
Biodiversity Importance:This urban ecosystem provides critical services including carbon sequestration, air purification, urban heat island reduction, and stormwater management. The species diversity creates habitat corridors for urban wildlife and offers mental health benefits for city residents.
Human Influence:
Positive impacts: Park maintenance preserves mature trees, creates wildlife habitat, and provides green space access
Negative impacts: Soil compaction from foot traffic, introduction of non-native turf species, and potential chemical inputs from maintenance
Ecosystem Importance: Urban parks like this serve as biodiversity refuges in developed landscapes, supporting both native species adaptation and human well-being while demonstrating sustainable coexistence between natural systems and urban development.
